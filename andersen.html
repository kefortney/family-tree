The Andersen branch is the paternal line of Jill Andersen Fortney. The line traces to Kragerø, Norway — a coastal town in Telemark county — and to Andrew Bernard Andersen Sr., who emigrated from Norway to Holland, Michigan in 1914, through Ellis Island. His son, Andrew B. Andersen II, married Shirley Mulhearn, connecting the Norwegian Andersen line with the Irish-American Mulhearn family and — through their daughter Jill's marriage to Alan Jon Fortney — ultimately converging all four family branches.

Andrew Sr.'s father was Ole Andersen, from the Kragerø area. His mother's maiden name was Mortland, from a neighboring Norwegian town. Andrew Sr. married Guerena Knutson, born in Wisconsin — her parents had themselves emigrated from Norway to Wisconsin a generation earlier.

Norwegian Origins — Kragerø

Kragerø is a small coastal town in Telemark, southeastern Norway, known for its archipelago of islands and its history as a trading port. The painter Edvard Munch spent time there; it was a well-known artists' retreat in the early 20th century. The Andersen family lived in this region before emigration.

Ole Andersen and his wife — maiden name Mortland, from a neighboring town — were the grandparents of Jill Andersen Fortney. Their son Andrew departed for America in 1914, just before World War I, when tens of thousands of Norwegians were heading to the Great Lakes states.

Andrew Bernard Andersen Sr. — The Emigrant

Andrew Bernard Andersen Sr. emigrated from Kragerø, Norway to Holland, Michigan in 1914, arriving through Ellis Island in New York City. Holland, Michigan — settled largely by Dutch immigrants in the 1840s but surrounded by Scandinavian communities — was his American home. He is the patriarch of the American Andersen line represented here.

In America, Andrew Sr. married Guerena Knutson, a Wisconsin-born woman of Norwegian descent. Her parents had made the crossing from Norway to Wisconsin a generation earlier, making Guerena first-generation American — part of the Norwegian communities that settled the Upper Midwest throughout the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Andrew B. Andersen II & Shirley Mulhearn

Andrew B. Andersen II was born on 7 August 1918 in Michigan — son of Andrew Sr. and Guerena Knutson. He married Shirley Mulhearn in 1948 — the fifth child of Robert and Agnes Mulhearn of Newark, New Jersey. Both are deceased. Andrew died in Texas; Shirley was born 20 April 1922 in New Jersey and died 4 March 1994 in New Jersey, buried in a Veterans Cemetery in New Jersey.

Andrew and Shirley met during World War II — he was serving with post-Normandy forces in Paris; she was in the Women's Auxiliary Corps and they met in England before his unit crossed to France.

Jill Andersen Fortney

Jill Andersen Fortney married Alan Jon Fortney, bringing together all four family branches represented on this site. She is an accomplished photographer — the Norway photographs in the gallery, taken during the 2005 visit to the ancestral Forthun farm, are credited to her.

Together with Alan, Jill met with Torill Susanne Magnusdotter Yttri Otterhjell, the current owner of the Forthun farm (Bnr #8), and helped verify and expand the genealogical record of the Norwegian branch.

Guerena Knutson — The Wisconsin Knutsons

Guerena Knutson was born in Wisconsin. Her parents were Norwegian immigrants who had settled in Wisconsin before her birth — part of the large Norwegian communities that took root across Wisconsin, Minnesota, and the Upper Midwest in the late 19th century. The Knutson name (from the Norwegian Knutsen, "son of Knut") is common throughout Norway and Norwegian-American communities alike.

Detailed records on Guerena's family — her parents' names and their Norwegian origins — are not yet documented.

The Norwegian Symmetry

There is a quiet symmetry in this family: both the Fortney / Forthun line and the Andersen line trace back to Norway. The Fortney line descends from Luster, in the western fjord country — a farm worked for over 450 years. The Andersen line comes from Kragerø, on the southeastern coast. They emigrated in different eras and settled in different parts of America. But in the next generation, in Wisconsin, their descendants found each other.

Andrew B. Andersen II and Shirley Mulhearn's daughter Jill marrying Alan Jon Fortney was the moment two Norwegian-origin lines — separated by geography, time, and the Atlantic — rejoined.
