The Farm at Forthun, Norway

Key Places

  • Forthun (Fortun), Luster parish
  • Sogn, Norway
  • Bruks Nummer 8 (Farm Bnr #8)
  • Fantofte Stavkirke, Bergen

The family name derives from Forthun (also spelled Fortun), a farm and village in Luster parish, Sogn, Norway. The Norwegian farm numbering system (bruks nummer, Bnr) tracked which parcel of land a family held. The Fortney line runs through Bnr #8.

The crucial event that established the family on Bnr #8 came in 1653, when Synneva Clausdotter married Knut Aamundson Forthun — bringing the farm parcel into the family. That parcel remained in family hands for 255 years, until 1908.

The earliest known ancestor is Aamund Jonson Forthun, born approximately 1570, who served as farm manager of the neighboring Bnr #1. His son Claus Aamundson (b. 1585) fathered Synneva, and it is through her that the Bnr #8 line descends.

"The Fortney family genealogy was traced back to 1616, which is remarkable considering that we were farmers. Compiling it has been the work of several generations." — Centennial Reunion 2007 presentation

The original Fortun Stave Church stood in the village until 1883, when it had grown too small for the congregation and was sold to Bergen merchants. It was relocated to Bergen and became the famous Fantofte Stavkirke — embellished with characteristic Viking-style carving. In 1992, arsonists destroyed it as part of a wave of church arsons in Norway. It was rebuilt and stands today as a major cultural landmark.

The Last Fortney on the Farm

The last family member to live on Bnr #8 was Kristina Olsdotter Fortun (b. 26 May 1877, d. 12 January 1939). When her emigrating brother Christen left for America in 1906, she received the deed from him on 29 January 1908. She managed the farm until around 1925, then earned a pension (kår) despite never marrying.

Finding Kristina's grave in the local kirkegård (churchyard), across the road from the Fortun village church, required considerable genealogical sleuthing. Alan Jon Fortney and Jill Andersen Fortney visited the farm in 2005 and confirmed the genealogy with current owner Torill Susanne Magnusdotter Yttri Otterhjell.

After Kristina left active farming, the farm was managed by the Optum brothers (Arne and Johannes), who sold it to Magnus Asbjørn Tollefson Yttri around February 7, 1958. Magnus's daughter Torill and her husband Andreas Otterhjell have been the farm's owners since March 7, 1979.

Emigration to America, 1853

The great Norwegian emigrations of the 1840s and 1850s emptied the Forthun valley of nearly 40% of its male population. Of Ole Knutson Forthun's seven children, five emigrated to America between 1848 and 1856:

  • Torger Olson (b. 1808) — emigrated 1848
  • Knut Olson (b. 1812) — emigrated 1856
  • Christen Olson (b. 1814) — emigrated 1856
  • Thomas Olson (b. 1817) — emigrated 1853 ★ our line
  • Ole Olson (b. 1822) — emigrated 1848

Thomas Olson Forthun (b. 9 February 1817, Forthun; d. 5 May 1887, Vernon County, WI) emigrated in 1853, settling first in Franklin County, then Vernon County, Wisconsin. He married Susanna Johannesdotter Ottum (b. 21 September 1835, Luster; d. 3 April 1892) — who had herself emigrated from Norway in 1850 — on 13 October 1854 in Dane County, Wisconsin.

Together they had eight children. The sixth child — Hans Thomassen Fortney — would become the patriarch of the American Fortney family.

Gen I (American) Hans Thomassen Fortney + Maria Johanna Swangstu b. 1861, d. 1941 · Wheeler, Wisconsin

Hans Thomassen Fortney (b. 18 February 1861, Vernon County, WI; d. 22 February 1941, Wheeler, WI) married Maria Johanna Swangstu (b. 20 November 1866, West Prairie, WI; d. 5 December 1945, Wheeler, WI) on 6 April 1887. In 1907 they moved the family to Wheeler, Wisconsin, which became the family's home base for generations.

Hans and Maria had ten children, born between 1889 and 1919. Two died young or without children; the remaining eight produced 26 grandchildren, 66 great-grandchildren, and over 100 great-great-grandchildren by the time of the 2007 Centennial Reunion.

Chaplain Major Albin Leonard Fortney

Albin Leonard Fortney (b. 10 July 1906, Viroqua, WI; d. 18 May 1968, Stoughton, WI) was the ninth of Hans and Maria's ten children. He became a Lutheran minister and, in 1939, an Army Chaplain — a career that would take his family around the world.

In his first parish in Cook, Minnesota (1935–37), sixty out-of-town guests arrived the first summer alone. His camp meal of choice — Squaw Corn (creamed corn fried over an open fire) — became a family legend.

Having discovered he was "enormously overweight" in Rocky River, Ohio, and recognizing that ministers were poorly paid, Albin joined the Army in 1939. He received his commission as Second Lieutenant on the very day his son Alan Jon Fortney was born — November 18, 1939. Alan's first crib was an opened bureau drawer.

Pearl Harbor

In 1941, Albin was in Hawaii awaiting the arrival of his family, scheduled for December 24. The Japanese attack of December 7, 1941 changed those plans: Albin was put in charge of burials, "of which there were more than desirable." His family stayed in Portland, Oregon — near Anita's brother, Rev. Carl Södergren.

World War II & Europe

Albin served in the European theater, reaching the rank of Major Chaplain. In 1945 he was part of Operation Midnight Sun — the U.S. Army's liberation of Norway from the Nazis. While in Norway, he had access to the parish Bible, an official record of the Fortun families, which provided crucial genealogical information. He credited his cousins Ruby and Alfred Fortney of Viroqua for supplying much of his genealogical research.

After the war, Albin re-enlisted on the condition that the whole family could accompany him to Europe. In February 1947, the family crossed the Atlantic in heavy winter storms aboard the USS Callan — Anita was not well; the boys were unbothered. They lived in Schweinfurt, Germany (at 7 Killiansberg, below the local mental hospital), then Vienna, Austria (23 Kassgraben Gasse).

In Vienna, Albin developed heart problems and was discharged at Carlisle Barracks. His brother-in-law Arthur Piepkorn, a Colonel, was stationed there at the time.

The Parish Years

Albin's pastoral career was marked by a recurring pattern: brilliant ministry, inevitable dispute, resignation or dismissal. His uncle Cliff foresaw this early; Albin's own assessment was that he was simply right when others were wrong. After parishes in Muskegon and North Muskegon, Michigan, and Stoughton and Tomahawk, Wisconsin, he left the ministry and sold Lutheran Brotherhood Insurance, then joined Social Security in Madison.

Loss

Albin lived to witness the burial of his youngest son, Kendall Thomas Fortney, killed in Vietnam on 6 February 1968. Due to bureaucratic error, Kendall was initially listed as Missing in Action. With the help of Senator William Proxmire and journalist Larry K. Knutsen, his body was properly identified and buried in Stoughton in March 1968.

Albin Leonard Fortney died "in the harness" on 18 May 1968, of colonic cancer — just ten days before their 33rd wedding anniversary. He had lived 61 years, 10 months, and 8 days.

Alan Jon Fortney

Alan Jon Fortney (b. 18 November 1939, Rocky River, OH) is the second son of Albin and Anita. He married Jill Andersen, and together they continued the family's genealogical research — most notably the 2005 trip to Norway, where Jill photographed the ancestral Forthun farm and Alan confirmed the genealogy with Torill Otterhjell.

Alan is the primary compiler of the family history documented on this site, incorporating research from multiple generations: his own research, his father's wartime notes from Norway, his cousin Adrian H. Dahl's acquisition of the authoritative Luster: Gards-og Ættesoge Fortun Sokns, and Agnes Fortney Deason and Sylvia Deason's thorough independent research.

Alan and Jill have two children: Rachael Fortney (b. 1971) and Kendall Fortney — artist and filmmaker, named for his uncle Kendall Thomas Fortney, KIA Vietnam 1968.

Research Notes & Gaps

  • No records exist for Jon, the father of Aamund Jonson (b. 1570) — he is the earliest verifiable ancestor.
  • Knut Aamundson's exact parentage has been questioned — dates suggest Aamund Jonson may not be his direct father; previous genealogies listed him as such, but ages don't quite fit.
  • Maria Johanna Swangstu's family line has not been documented beyond her birth in West Prairie, WI.
  • The descendants of Thomas Olson Forthun's other children (Ole, Johannes, Anna, Arne, Thomas, Christine) have not been traced.
  • Several living descendants are noted as (bd) — birth date — with details withheld for privacy.
