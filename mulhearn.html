Mulhearn / O'Brien Branch

The Mulhearn & O'Brien Family

Irish Origins · County Clare & County Donegal · Newark, New Jersey

Three Irish Lines

Three Converging Lines

  • Mulhearn — County Clare, Ireland
  • McEntee — County Donegal, Ireland
  • O'Brien — County Clare, Ireland

All three emigrated to Newark, New Jersey in the mid-19th century, likely during the Great Hunger. Their American-born descendants converge at Robert Mulhearn and Agnes McEntee of Newark.

The American Mulhearn clan descends from three Irish immigrant lines that converged in Newark, New Jersey: the Mulhearn line, the McEntee line, and the O'Brien line.

Research compiled by Donna Hefner Martin, with contributions from Alan Jon Fortney, Patricia McGroary, and family members. A family book was being compiled by Donna Hefner Martin as of 2016.

The Names

Mulhearn — County Clare

Mulhearn originates in County Clare, Ireland. William Mulhearn emigrated from Ballyshannon — the specific townland has been confirmed, which is fortunate given how many Irish records were destroyed in the 1922 Dublin fire. Some family accounts suggest the Irish relatives may have come from County Roscommon, though this is uncertain and may refer to a different branch of the family; further research is needed.

McEntee — County Donegal

McEntee (Gaelic: Mac an tSaoi, "Son of the Scholar") is a storied clan originally from County Donegal — Dún na nGall, "the Place of the Strangers," a reference to the Vikings who once settled the coast. The clan is known for territorial disputes with neighbors. As Patricia McGroary noted, some historians suggest the "scholar" epithet was applied with a degree of irony — they were not always scholarly, but they were reliably troublesome.

O'Brien — Descended from Brian Boru

O'Brien (Gaelic: Ua Briain, "descendant of Brian") is the sixth most common surname in Ireland. The O'Briens are descended from Brian Boru (941–1014), who became King of Thomond in AD 976. Thomond encompassed most of modern County Clare — meaning both the Mulhearn and O'Brien lines likely originate from the same region.

Castle Garden — How They Came to America

The Irish ancestors in this family — Mulhearn, McEntee, and O'Brien — almost certainly arrived through Castle Garden, not Ellis Island. Castle Garden (formally the Castle Garden Emigrant Landing Depot) operated at the southern tip of Manhattan from 1855 to 1890, and was the primary entry point for immigrants to America before Ellis Island opened in 1892.

An 1850s arrival during The Great Hunger (An Gorta Mór, 1845–1852) — the most likely window for their emigration — would place them squarely in the Castle Garden era. Between 1845 and 1855, over one million Irish died of starvation and disease, and another million emigrated. Many settled in the port cities where they arrived: New York, Boston, and the surrounding areas. Newark, New Jersey, directly across the harbor from Manhattan, was a natural destination.

Castle Garden records were later destroyed by fire, which is why documentary evidence for this generation is difficult or impossible to locate. What we know comes from family tradition and circumstantial evidence.

The Immigrant Generation

Philip and Ann McEntee emigrated from Ireland and settled in the Newark area, where they had a son, Bernard Sylvester McEntee.

Patrick O'Brien was born in 1812 in County Clare and emigrated to America, settling in Newark, New Jersey. He lived "Down Neck" at 43 Broad Street — the neighborhood now known as the Ironbound — and farmed the salt marshes east of his home (today's Meadowlands). He later kept a saloon. Patrick and his wife Margaret had a daughter, Sarah O'Brien (b. 26 December 1847, Newark).

Unresolved question: Patricia McGroary noted that the generational math is uncertain — if Sarah was born in 1847, Patrick may be her grandfather rather than father, with William O'Brian as the intermediate generation. Sarah's mother may have been a Noonan or Scanlon. The 1922 Dublin fire destroyed the records that might have clarified this.

Bernard Sylvester McEntee married Sarah O'Brien. Their daughter, Agnes McEntee, became the matriarch of the American Mulhearn clan. Both Bernard McEntee and Sarah O'Brien are believed to have been born in the United States — second-generation Irish-Americans by the time of Agnes's birth.

William Mulhearn & the Mulhearn Line

William Mulhearn emigrated from Ballyshannon, County Clare — likely during or just after the Great Hunger in the 1850s. His son, Robert Mulhearn Sr., was born in the United States, possibly in New Jersey.

Robert Mulhearn Sr. (who carried a different middle initial from his son of the same name) married Sarah Gans, an Austrian Jewish immigrant born in the United States. This is an unexpected pairing for an Irish Catholic family, but not unheard of in the immigrant communities of 19th-century Newark, where different groups lived in close quarters. The details of Robert Sr.'s life — exact birth date, middle name, and where he met Sarah Gans — are to be confirmed from family records currently being assembled.

Robert & Agnes Mulhearn — Newark, New Jersey

Robert Mulhearn (son of Robert Mulhearn Sr. and Sarah Gans) was born in Newark, New Jersey. He married Agnes McEntee, also born in Newark. Together they had ten children.

Research Notes

  • County Roscommon has been mentioned as a possible origin for some Irish relatives, but County Clare (Mulhearn) and County Donegal (McEntee) are the confirmed origins from existing research. The Roscommon connection may refer to a different branch and will be explored in the forthcoming family booklet.
  • The details of Robert Mulhearn Sr.'s life — and his marriage to Sarah Gans — are to be confirmed from family records currently being assembled.
