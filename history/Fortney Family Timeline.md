# Albin Leonard and Anita Linnea Södergren Fortney
## Family Timeline

*Written by Alan Jon Fortney, incorporating material Steven added for his website (2009) and earlier family records.*

---

**10 July 1906** — Albin Leonard Fortney born in Viroqua, Wisconsin.

**17 February 1907** — Anita Linnea Södergren born in Moline, Illinois; the family moved to Burlington, Iowa.

**28 May 1935** — Marriage. Minneapolis, Minnesota.

---

**1935–37 — Cook, Minnesota.** 1st parish. They lived in a tiny filling station — a Pure station, as befits a man of the cloth. The first summer they had 60 out-of-town guests. Albin's camp meal of choice was Squaw Corn: creamed corn fried in a frying pan over an open fire.

> **Son #1: Steven Dale Fortney** born 2 January 1937, in Minneapolis. Anita didn't trust yokel doctors. Albin must have loved her, for the trip in the dead of winter in 1937 was arduous.

---

**1937–38 — Rogers, North Dakota.** Had a three-point parish, left over from a six-point parish. Part of his salary was in produce — a sack of potatoes, a sack of flour. Left under unclear circumstances. Albin said he wasn't making enough money to live on; his portion of the divided parish was the poorest. Uncle Cliff suggested it was a dispute of some sort, because they always seemed to crop up. Albin went to Detroit with the expectation he would work with his brother, Thomas Kendahl, as a social worker — but got a call to a church in Rocky River, Ohio before he settled in.

---

**1938–1939 — Rocky River, Ohio.** T'was here Albin discovered how enormously overweight he was. That, and the knowledge that Hitler was causing a ruckus — and that ministers were poorly paid — prompted him to join the army.

> **Son #2: Alan Jon Fortney** born 18 November 1939 — on the very day Albin received his commission as a second lieutenant in the US Army. Alan's first crib was an opened bureau drawer.

---

**1939 — Charleston, South Carolina.** Albin went through basic training. Steven, because of the family's Black nanny, Willa Mae Barnes, developed a deep southern accent: *"Mamua, de dox aw gowan."* He also heard from her many scary stories about the 'bookie man.'

---

**1940 — Boston, Massachusetts.** Albin attended chaplain's school, then was posted to Fort Edward, New York (once called Fort Niagara).

**1940–1941 — Fort Edward, New York.**

> **Son #3: Kendall Thomas Fortney** born 2 June 1941. The family had to wait for Albin's next posting, because Kendall had to be at least six months old for the army to move the family by ship to Hawaii.

---

**1941 — Honolulu, Hawaii.** Albin was in Hawaii awaiting the arrival of his family, scheduled for 24 December. The Japanese attack on 7 December 1941 forced a change of plans and priorities; Albin was put in charge of burials, of which there were more than desirable.

---

**1941–1943 — Portland, Oregon.** Anita received a garbled cable from Albin advising her to "keep the appointment," followed quickly by the corrected one telling her to keep the apartment. She and the three boys stayed here, near her brother, Rev. Carl Södergren and his family.

---

**1943 — Muskogee, Oklahoma.** Albin was posted to Fort Sill (Apache territory). The whole family together again. Alan's toe was bitten by a huge fire ant — first experience with Epsom salts. Steven broke his arm swinging from the rafters of a friend's garage (Alan cried on his behalf). Steven had his tonsils out. Kendall was still only a toe-headed toddler.

---

**1944–1945 — Menomonie, Wisconsin.** Family moved back to the comfortable Midwest; Albin was sent to the European theater of World War II. Three houses here:
1. A rented cottage on Tainter Lake, where the boys were bribed with ice-cream cones to choke down cream of mushroom soup — and ate the first fish they caught: a sucker.
2. The borrowed Venes house (belonging to Virgil Fortney's in-laws), where Alan alienated the next-door-neighbor boy.
3. The house Anita bought without Albin's input in Menomonie itself, where Phoebe said of one of their chickens, *"Now that's what I call a dumb cluck,"* because it laid no eggs one day.

Albin visited once on leave and shot a flare gun off into the dark night sky.

---

**1945–1947 — Tacoma, Washington.** Albin returned from Europe; the war was over and he was stationed at Fort Lewis. The family lived off-post on Meadow Road. The boys — especially Kendall — embarked on a criminal career by holding up the GIs while Albin conducted choir practice.

> **Daughter #1 (and only): Anita Linnea Fortney** born 3 July 1947. Anita often remarked that she had four children in four different cities and four different states — and indicated she didn't need more.

---

**1947–1948 — Schweinfurt, Germany.** Albin had re-upped in the Army, now at the rank of Major Chaplain, on the condition the whole family could go to Europe. The family crossed the Atlantic in February storms aboard the USS *Callan* — Anita was not well. Steven cut his peas in half before trying to eat them, and threw up anyway. Kendall, Alan, and Albin were unaffected by the heavy winter seas. They lived just below the mental hospital at 7 Killiansberg, which Steven revisited in 1996 and Alan in June 2008.

---

**1948–1949 — Vienna, Austria.** Lived at 23 Kassgraben Gasse. Albin's second posting in Europe. He developed heart problems and was sent back to the United States for a formal discharge at Carlisle Barracks in Pennsylvania. Uncle (a colonel) Arthur and Aunt Miriam Piepkorn lived there at the time. The boys caught fireflies with Mary, Faith, Felicity, and Angela.

---

**1949 — Baldwin, Wisconsin.** The family camped at Uncle Fred and Aunt Nettie Dahl's home until the church could find Albin a call. Alan started 5th grade here, with his bald-headed girlfriend. Steven threw an apple at a robin and almost killed it. He also went fishing with Uncle Fred.

---

**1949–1953 — Muskegon, Michigan.** Albin received a call to First Lutheran Church. He drove there in a snow storm and almost slid the car into a telephone pole — arriving on Alan's 10th birthday. The family lived at 233 Giddings Avenue. The congregation was in the midst of building a new church on Leonard Street; Albin changed the plan and decided to build the church in North Muskegon instead.

---

**1953–55 — North Muskegon, Michigan.** The interim church was in the roller rink next to the new building site. Albin affected Morning Suit and Homburg most Sundays. The Church in the Pines was finished. A dispute with the Lundgrens et al. led to his resignation — shades of Uncle Cliff's pronouncement.

---

**1955–1957 — Stoughton, Wisconsin.** Albin was called to Our Savior's Lutheran Church. Prospect Street house — ten rooms and a silo. Steven attended the University of Wisconsin. Alan graduated from high school. Tension within two years led to this, his third disputatious parish, and the family was about to move again. Alan told Dad he was staying to graduate high school no matter what Dad was going to do. The family moved two days after the graduation ceremony.

---

**1957–1959 — Tomahawk, Wisconsin.** Albin's last parish. Again disputes caused dismissal or, perhaps, resignation — it was never clear. What was clear is that Albin always thought he was right, while his congregants did not always agree with his inerrancy. By this time, Uncle Cliff's unflattering prognostications were holding true, in spades.

---

**1959 — until end — Stoughton, Wisconsin.** Albin sold Lutheran Brotherhood Insurance and bought a used Cadillac, then got a job with Social Security in Madison.

> **Albin Leonard Fortney** died "in the harness" on **18 May 1968**, as a result of colonic cancer — 10 days before their 33rd wedding anniversary. He lived 61 years, 10 months, and 8 days.

He lived long enough to witness the burial of his youngest son, **Kendall Thomas Fortney**, killed in Vietnam on **6 February 1968**. Due to bureaucratic bungling, Kendall Thomas was erroneously listed as MIA for a month until, with the help of Senator William Proxmire and journalist Larry K. Knutsen, his body was properly identified, sent home, and buried in Stoughton in March 1968. Albin lived only about eight weeks beyond that.

> **Anita Linnea Södergren Fortney** remained in the house on North Street (their 25th home) until she went into Skaalen Home, where she died on **4 July 1990** — before breakfast — after 22 years, 2 months, and 6 days as a widow. She lived 84 years, 4 months, and 15 days.

---

*This timeline cites 19 separate homes, so 6 are missing. Family legend has it Mom set up house 25 times by the time she and Dad celebrated their 25th anniversary.*

## Surviving Children and Descendants

Albin and Anita's two sons and one daughter survive — all of whom outlived Albin Leonard.

**Steven** has four children (3 daughters, one son), **Alan** two (one daughter, one son), and **Anita** one daughter.

Steven has 9 grandchildren, only two carrying the Fortney name [Alexander and Christine Marsh's Jack and Henry Fortney]:

- Sigrid and Christopher Alekal's daughter Alisha Blue and a son Cyrus Hauck

Plus one great-granddaughter from Emily and Matthew Bissen.
